After years of studying Chinese, I still find myself tangled up in how to talk about experience. If I want to say I have experienced something, my first instinct is _我经历过这个_, but that never feels quite right. Maybe _我有这种经历_ is better. And then there are all the other words: _经验_, _体验_. I know I can say _按照我的经验_ or _按照我的体验_, but beyond that, I have never really understood how to use them.

I have decided it is time to stop being confused. I want to sort this out on my own terms, in a way that makes sense to me—not through abstract explanations, but through a framework I can actually hold onto.

Let me start with _经历_. This one seems straightforward. It works as both a verb and a noun, and it maps most directly onto the English "to experience." But what kind of experience? I think of it as experience as story. Experience, in this sense, is what consciousness produces—feelings, perceptions—but once those moments are woven together, they form a narrative. _经历_ is the story that comes out of what you have lived. So if I am ever unsure, I will default to _经历_.

Then there is _经验_. From what I have read, this is about skills and knowledge. It is less the raw experience itself and more the conclusion drawn from it. If _经历_ is the story, _经验_ is the lesson. It is the result, the takeaway, the thing you carry forward.

_体验_ is different again. It feels closer to "try" or "sample." It often carries a sense of novelty—doing something for the first time, or immersing yourself in something deliberately. It is still personal, still rooted in feeling, but it emphasizes the moment-to-moment act of experiencing rather than the story that accumulates. That is why it gets confused with _经历_, but I think the distinction lies in temporality: _体验_ is the conscious process in real time, while _经历_ is the retrospective whole.

And yet there are even more words that orbit this territory.

_感受_ is the closest to "to feel." I use it when I am actively taking in a sensory or emotional experience.  
_我想去海边感受一下海风。_  
_她闭上眼睛，感受音乐里的每一个音符。_  
_站在山顶，我能感受到一种说不出的自由。_

_体会_ is experience that leads to understanding. It is what happens when you go through something and, through that process, come to grasp something deeply.  
_只有自己带过孩子，才能真正体会父母的辛苦。_  
_他在沙漠里住了三个月，体会到了水的珍贵。_  
_有些道理年轻时听再多也没用，得自己体会。_

_体味_ is slower, more deliberate. It implies savoring—lingering in an experience long enough to taste its nuances.  
_他坐在老街上，慢慢体味这座城市的旧时光。_  
_有些诗需要反复读，才能体味其中的孤独。_

_体验_ reappears here as well, but in its own register:  
_我想体验一下当老师的感觉。_  
_他报名去农村住一周，就是为了体验不同的生活。_  
_戴上VR眼镜，你可以体验在太空行走的感觉。_

And finally, _感知_, which is more perceptual and intellectual—experiencing as becoming aware, often of something subtle or abstract.  
_婴儿通过触摸来感知世界。_  
_我能隐约感知到他和平时不太一样。_

After all this, I come back to _经历_. It remains the closest thing to a true equivalent of "experience." The others are not wrong—they are just more specific. They carve out particular aspects of what it means to experience: feeling, understanding, savoring, sampling, perceiving.

What becomes clear is that even in English, "experience" does double duty. It refers to both the ongoing process of being conscious of something and the accumulated story that results from it. If I do not distinguish between these two even in my own mind, I will keep running into confusion when I try to move between English and Chinese. The problem is not just about finding the right word. It is about knowing which facet of experience I am actually trying to name.
